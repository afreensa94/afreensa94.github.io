SpellStep

Task initiation scaffolding for neurodivergent users — designed through 8 research methods.

Overview

Neurodivergent students and professionals often struggle with task initiation — not due to laziness, but due to cognitive overwhelm, perfectionism, and emotional friction that makes starting feel impossible. SpellStep reimagines productivity through story-first design, transforming tasks into a gentle narrative journey supported by emotional cues, an AI companion, and kindness-based motivation.

This case study documents the full research process: 8 methods, 5 user interviews, 2 rounds of prototype testing, a tree test, an open card sort, heuristic evaluations of existing apps, and literature grounded in Self-Determination Theory.

The Problem

Existing focus apps like Forest and Focus Quest were built around neurotypical assumptions — punishment-based streaks, reward-heavy systems, and cluttered UIs that create more cognitive load than they reduce. For ADHD and AuDHD users, these systems backfire: missed streaks become shame, long task lists become paralysis, and the app becomes another thing they're failing at.

Our interviews confirmed this. Participants described large assignments as "paralyzing" because they didn't know how to begin. They procrastinated not out of laziness but out of confusion about the first step. They relied on classmates and deadlines — not apps — to stay on track.

Research Methods

Method 1 — Literature Review

Foundational · 3 peer-reviewed articles

We reviewed articles on gamified productivity, SDT, and ADHD cognitive regulation. Key finding: reward-heavy systems can reduce intrinsic motivation in users who are already overwhelmed. Apps work best when they support autonomy and emotional safety — not when they pressure users with badges, points, or punishments. The "focus paradox" was also documented: apps designed to help you focus can themselves become a source of distraction when overly complex. This grounded our shift away from heavy gamification toward emotional clarity.

Method 2 — User Interviews

Qualitative · 5 participants · ADHD / AuDHD

We interviewed 5 neurodivergent students. Three sessions were fully in-person; two used a hybrid format — Zoom Q&A followed by in-person usability testing of existing apps. Each session covered: a semi-structured interview about task initiation and emotional experiences, a walkthrough of Forest and Focus Quest, and a post-task emotional reflection. Sessions were recorded, transcribed in Google Docs, and analyzed via affinity diagramming in Figma.

Consistent themes across all 5 participants:

  • Overwhelm from complex, multi-step tasks — described as "paralyzing"
  • Procrastination driven by confusion about the first step, not avoidance
  • Pattern of starting strong, then losing focus mid-task without external structure
  • Perfectionist tendencies that blocked initiation entirely
  • Emotional overwhelm tied to task visibility — seeing the full list increased anxiety
  • Reliance on classmates and deadlines rather than apps for accountability

During existing app testing, participants expressed genuine guilt when their Forest plant died and found Focus Quest's dialogue "annoying rather than motivating" — reactions that directly shaped SpellStep's no-punishment design principle.

Method 3 — Heuristic Evaluation

Competitive Analysis · Forest & Focus Quest · UI Tenets & Trap Cards

Key issues found across both apps:

  • Focus Quest: too many steps before starting, duplicate cues for the same action, rewards disconnected from real progress
  • Forest: unclear affordances, no centralized home screen, punitive visual when a plant died, unintuitive timer restart flow
  • Both apps made users rely on memory rather than guiding them with clear cues
  • Neither app had adequate emotional support — only emotional pressure

Method 4 — Cognitive Walkthrough

Competitive Analysis · Forest & Focus Quest

We performed first-time-user walkthroughs of both apps, logging confusion points at each step. Focus Quest required too many choices upfront. Forest's timer restart was unintuitive. Both created cognitive debt in users before any actual work began. This confirmed that SpellStep needed simple paths, fewer steps, and zero guilt mechanics from the very first interaction.

Method 5 — Tree Testing

Information Architecture · 10 tasks · UXArmy

We ran a 10-task tree test with classmates to validate SpellStep's navigation structure before building the prototype. Tasks covered the full feature set: goal setting, task chunking, companion customization, progress map, and timer controls.

Overall success rate: 86.25% — users could find the right location most of the time, confirming the navigation foundation was sound.

Directness rate: 67.5% — users needed extra clicks or backtracking, indicating some labels didn't match mental models.

Task 10 (edit companion personality): 37% success rate — worst performer. Narrative feature labels (Story, Character, Quests) caused confusion because they felt too similar without visual support.

Structural, action-oriented features (timers, goals, chunking) performed best. Emotional and tone-based features needed clearer, simpler naming — finding that directly shaped our section labels.

Method 6 — Open Card Sort

Information Architecture · 24 cards · Optimal Workshop

We ran an open card sort with classmates and peers completing the activity remotely. Participants sorted 24 cards representing SpellStep's features and labeled their own groups. We added post-task reflection questions after noticing early confusion, including an emotional state check ("How did you feel while sorting?") and questions about unclear or overlapping cards.

  • 78–92% agreement: Planning, Steps, Rewards, Focus Sessions — grouped nearly identically across participants
  • 40–60% agreement: Progress tracking — placed under motivation, tasks, or rewards with no clear consensus
  • 12–25% agreement: Emotional support cards (encouragement, calming messages, self-talk) — highest confusion, lowest agreement
  • 56% of participants (9/16) marked at least 3 cards as hard to place due to unclear wording or overlapping meanings

User-created labels — "Getting Started," "Mini-Steps," "Daily Flow," "Motivation," "Focus Tools" — directly informed SpellStep's section naming. Emotional support features needed the most rework.

Method 7 — Prototype Usability Testing

Usability · 2 rounds · Figma · 4 participants total

Two rounds of testing with a clickable Figma prototype. Participants completed 6 core tasks: entering a goal, reviewing broken-down steps, prioritizing tasks, locking tasks, navigating to the progress map, and interpreting companion guidance.

Round 1 (1 participant — rapid iteration):

  • Overall app intent and task chunking logic were understood
  • Priority star icon not recognized without supporting context
  • Companion pet wasn't noticed — no visual cues signaled its presence
  • Duplicate "Begin Quest" button on the breakdown page caused confusion

Changes applied: added pet icons, improved button labels, added clear lock and star icons with better affordance.

Round 2 (3 participants — validation):

  • Overall flow (Onboarding → Choose Pet → Breakdown → Progress) made sense to all 3
  • Task chunking and lock feature conceptually understood
  • "Decide for Me" not understood by any participant — 0% comprehension
  • Pet dialogues felt vague and unhelpful — users wanted actionable guidance
  • Task Breakdown page felt incomplete; users wanted sub-steps within each chunked task
  • Progress Map lacked context — users didn't understand what they were looking at
  • Prioritization arrows were missed; star icon still didn't immediately read as "priority"

Method 8 — Personas & Journey Mapping

Synthesis · Figma & Miro

We built two personas from our combined research: Sharon (the "before" state — overwhelm, task overload, avoidance) and Alex (the "after" — guided steps, emotional reassurance, small wins). Journey maps in Miro visualized the emotional arc from guilt and perfectionism through gentle nudges, visual chunking, and eventual task completion. Both personas were used as reference checkpoints throughout design decisions.

Design Decisions Traced to Research

  • Task chunking into "spell steps" — interview finding: users didn't know how to start, not that they lacked motivation
  • No punishment for inactivity — SDT literature + Forest usability: guilt-based mechanics reduce motivation in ND users
  • Ambient companion over dialogue popups — tree test + prototype Round 2: intrusive dialogue annoyed users
  • Visual progress map instead of checklist — card sort: "progress" had no single mental model; narrative framing gave it emotional meaning
  • Renamed emotional support features — card sort: 12–25% agreement on tone-based labels; simplified to plain language
  • "Decide for Me" removed and reframed — prototype Round 2: 0% comprehension; needed complete rethink of affordance and label

Key Learnings

  • Emotional features are the hardest to name — structural labels (timer, steps, goals) are intuitive; emotional UX labels need plain language and visual support
  • Punishment-avoidance is a design requirement, not a nice-to-have — for ND users, guilt mechanics don't motivate; they cause abandonment
  • Rapid iteration with 1 participant reveals major issues — Round 1's single-participant test exposed the pet and icon problems that Round 2 confirmed as fixed
  • Tree testing before prototyping saves time — catching label confusion in the navigation structure early prevented those same issues appearing in the prototype
  • Card sort user labels are design language — "Getting Started" and "Mini-Steps" came from participants, not the team; listening to those words made the app feel more natural
