The Problem

Existing focus apps are built for neurotypical users. For ADHD and AuDHD users, punishment-based streaks and cluttered UIs don't motivate — they cause shame, avoidance, and abandonment.

User Research

5 semi-structured interviews with ADHD and AuDHD students. Sessions included app walkthroughs and post-task emotional reflection. Insights were synthesized via affinity diagramming.

Affinity diagram — 6 themes across 5 participants

Key finding

Participants procrastinated not out of laziness but confusion about the first step. The barrier was initiation, not motivation.

Key finding

All participants preferred people over apps for accountability. No existing tool felt emotionally safe enough to return to after a missed day.

Additional Methods

Beyond interviews and journey mapping, the research included competitive analysis, IA testing, and two rounds of prototype validation.

Heuristic Evaluation

Evaluated Forest and Focus Quest using UI Tenets & Trap Cards. Forest's plant-death mechanic caused genuine guilt. Focus Quest's rewards felt irrelevant to actual work.

Tree Testing — UXArmy

86.25% success rate, but directness dropped to 67.5%. Task 10 (edit companion personality) failed at 37% — narrative labels confused users without visual support.

Open Card Sort — Optimal Workshop

24 cards, remote participants. Structural features (steps, timers, goals) had 78–92% agreement. Emotional support features scored 12–25% — users had no shared mental model for them.

Prototype Testing — 2 Rounds

Round 1 (1 participant) exposed missing pet icons and a confusing duplicate button. Round 2 (3 participants) confirmed fixes — and revealed "Decide for Me" had 0% comprehension.

Design Decisions

Every feature traces back to a specific research finding.

  • Task chunking into "spell steps" — interviews: users were stuck on the first step, not unmotivated
  • No punishment for inactivity — SDT literature + Forest testing: guilt mechanics caused abandonment in ND users
  • Plain-language emotional labels — card sort: emotional support features scored lowest (12–25% agreement) — users needed simpler, more predictable naming
  • Ambient companion over dialogue popups — prototype Round 2: intrusive dialogue was "annoying rather than motivating"
  • "Decide for Me" removed — prototype Round 2: 0% comprehension across 3 participants
  • Visual progress map — card sort: "progress" had no consistent mental model; narrative framing made it emotionally legible

Key Learnings

  • Emotional features are the hardest to name — structural labels are intuitive; tone-based features need plain language and visual anchors
  • Rapid 1-participant testing reveals the biggest issues fastest — Round 1 exposed the pet and icon problems before they compounded
  • Punishment-avoidance is a design requirement for ND users — not a nice-to-have. Guilt mechanics don't motivate; they cause abandonment
  • Card sort user labels are better than team-invented ones — "Getting Started" and "Mini-Steps" came from participants, not us
