An interactive e-learning course unpacking the systems behind forced displacement.

Overview

Nuances of Forced Migration is an interactive e-learning course designed to unpack the how, why, and systems that fuel forced displacement across the globe. Unlike traditional educational resources that focus on surface-level causes, this course dives into the layered complexities of forced migration — including conflict, persecution, climate disasters, economic hardship, and the policies that trap people in motion.

Crafted to feel accessible yet emotionally resonant, this course blends UI/UX storytelling, real-world data, ELSI Principles, and intuitive learning modules to educate high school and college-level audiences on the multidimensional nature of migration.

Design Goals

  • Simplify complexity without compromising depth
  • Apply ELSI Principles to the topic
  • Represent lived experiences with dignity and nuance
  • Create a UI/UX that feels conversational, modular, and digestible
  • Empower users to think critically about systems — not just symptoms
  • Build a framework for layered learning: from personal impact to global

My Contributions

  • Designed UI wireframes and visual flow for modular navigation, including sidebar transitions, onboarding entry point, and progressive module reveal
  • Wrote and structured introductory module language to establish tone and contextual framing
  • Applied ELSI principles to make the lesson easier to process and help evoke empathy
  • Developed a scenario-based branching narrative system within select modules that let learners step into the role of displaced individuals
  • Built consistent narrative framing across modules to center empathy, agency, and systems-level understanding
  • Conducted internal usability tests to assess for information overload, emotional fatigue, and navigational clarity
  • Created text-to-visual mappings and illustrative graphic elements to reduce cognitive load while increasing retention

Evaluation Methodology

Learning outcomes were tested using a pre/post-test design with 6 participants. Each participant completed a questionnaire before and after the course to measure knowledge gain and empathy shift across three learning objectives.

Recruitment

Participants were recruited from the HCI 520 cohort — graduate students with varying prior knowledge of forced migration. The sample was intentionally kept small to respect time constraints, with the trade-off of limited statistical generalizability.

Instruments

  • Pre-test (8 questions): Multiple choice, mapped to LO1 (foundational knowledge of forced migration causes and affected populations)
  • Post-test (10 questions): Multiple choice + Likert scale (confidence rating 1–5) + open-ended reflection question, mapped to LO1, LO2, and LO3
  • The Likert and open-ended questions were added after the initial iteration — early testing revealed the instrument needed more introspective depth to capture empathy shifts

Findings

Knowledge (LO1)

Most participants entered with pre-existing foundational knowledge. Post-test responses revealed nuanced shifts rather than dramatic gains — for example, one participant shifted from "All of the above" to a more specific cause (persecution and political instability), suggesting the course deepened rather than introduced understanding. Some question-level variance pointed to areas where content clarity could be improved.

Emotional Understanding (LO2)

The scenario-based Module 5 — which placed learners in the role of a displaced individual facing survival decisions — was the primary driver of LO2 outcomes. Post-test responses showed a shift from factual recall to personal and emotional reflection.

Empathy & Behavioral Insight (LO3)

2 out of 6 participants explicitly expressed empathy in their open-ended post-test responses. Several expressed a desire to create safer spaces for displaced people — a behavioral intention shift that went beyond the course's stated knowledge objectives.

Limitations

  • Small sample size (n=6) limits statistical significance and generalizability
  • Convenience sampling from one cohort may not reflect diverse learner populations
  • Self-reported empathy is difficult to validate — responses reflect stated intention, not observed behavior

Key Learnings

  • Scenario-based learning drives affective outcomes — Module 5's choice-based immersion produced the strongest empathy responses, outperforming informational modules
  • Instrument design is iterative — The initial questionnaire lacked depth for measuring empathy; adding Likert + open-ended questions mid-process was the right call
  • Cognitive load and emotional load must be balanced — Keeping the course to 15–30 min with segmented modules prevented fatigue on a heavy topic
  • Pre-existing knowledge shapes learning goals — With an informed audience, the design goal shifted from teaching facts to deepening perspective
